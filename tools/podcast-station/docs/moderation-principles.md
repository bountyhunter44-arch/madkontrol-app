# Moderation Principles

## Purpose

Moderation should protect the quality and dignity of debate without turning the platform into an opaque viewpoint filter.

## Principles

1. Human dignity first.
2. Disagreement is allowed.
3. Abuse is not the same as disagreement.
4. Uncertainty must be visible.
5. Moderation labels should be explainable.
6. Human appeal or review must be possible for consequential decisions.
7. AI moderation is advisory unless explicitly approved otherwise in a future phase.

## What Moderation Should Detect

- Harassment.
- Threats.
- Dehumanizing language.
- Deliberate derailment.
- Source fabrication.
- Repeated refusal to engage with corrections.
- Manipulative framing that hides assumptions.
- False certainty from AI or humans.

## What Moderation Should Not Do

- Penalize a position only because it is unpopular.
- Force consensus.
- Hide uncertainty to make the debate feel cleaner.
- Treat AI judgment as final.
- Rank participants by political identity.

## Intervention Ladder

1. Gentle clarification prompt.
2. Request for evidence or restatement.
3. Turn-balancing nudge.
4. Moderator review flag.
5. Temporary pause.
6. Removal from public output after human review.

## AI Moderator Limits

AI may say:

- "This claim needs evidence."
- "This wording may be personal rather than policy-focused."
- "I may be missing context."
- "A human moderator should review this."

AI must not say:

- "This side is correct."
- "This participant is bad."
- "The debate is settled."
- "No reasonable person could disagree."
